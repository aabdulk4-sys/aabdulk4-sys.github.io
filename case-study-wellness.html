UX/UI Case Study · Mobile App

MindBridge
Wellness App

Designing a mental health companion app that makes therapy resources accessible, personalized, and stigma-free for college students aged 18–24.

Role
Lead UX/UI Designer
Timeline
10 Weeks
Platform
iOS Mobile App
Tools
Figma, Maze, Miro
The Problem

College students avoid mental health support — even when they need it.

Despite 73% of college students experiencing a mental health crisis during their studies, only 35% seek professional help. Research interviews with 12 UNCC students revealed three core barriers: stigma around openly seeking help, long campus counseling waitlists averaging 3 weeks, and the overwhelming nature of generic wellness apps that felt impersonal and clinical.

Existing apps like Calm and Headspace focus on meditation, not active mental health support. Students needed something that felt like a trusted friend — not a symptom checker. My goal was to design an app that lowered the barrier to entry through personalization, anonymity controls, and a warm, non-clinical interface.

"I know I should talk to someone, but the counseling waitlist is weeks long, and I don't want my friends to see me using a therapy app." — Interview participant, age 20

Research & Discovery

What the data told me

I conducted 12 semi-structured user interviews and a survey with 68 respondents across UNCC's student population. I also ran a competitive audit of 4 existing wellness apps, evaluating them against Jakob Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics. The data painted a consistent picture.

The most critical insight: students didn't want to be diagnosed. They wanted to feel heard, track their mood privately, and get pointed toward real resources when they were ready — without judgment.

Design Process

From sketches to a system

I began with low-fidelity paper wireframes exploring three navigation models — bottom tab bar, card stack, and gesture-based. After a guerrilla usability test with 5 participants, the bottom tab bar won clearly: it matched users' existing mental models from apps like Instagram and Spotify.

Key design decisions included: a private "journal mode" with biometric lock, a mood check-in that took under 30 seconds, resource cards surfaced by mood (not browsed manually), and a warm color palette deliberately chosen to feel different from clinical blue-and-white health apps.

Testing & Iteration

Testing with real users, iterating on real feedback

I ran two rounds of moderated usability testing using Maze — 8 participants in round one, 6 in round two. I assigned 4 tasks: complete onboarding, log a mood entry, find a breathing exercise, and locate campus counseling resources.

Round one revealed two critical issues: 3 of 8 users couldn't find the journaling feature (buried two levels deep), and the onboarding felt "too long" — averaging 4.2 minutes when users expected under 2. In round two after addressing both, task completion improved from 62% to 94% and average onboarding time dropped to 1.8 minutes.

Final Product

A mental health companion students actually want to use

The final MindBridge design delivers a 4-screen onboarding, a 30-second daily mood check-in, a biometrically locked journal, mood-matched resource cards, and a direct campus counseling booking flow — all within a warm, non-clinical interface that tested as "approachable" and "private-feeling" by 9 of 11 final participants.
