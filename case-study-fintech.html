UX/UI Case Study · FinTech Dashboard

FinTrack
Spending Dashboard

Redesigning a personal finance dashboard for a FinTech startup to help young adults aged 22–35 understand their spending patterns and build better financial habits.

Role
UX/UI Designer
Timeline
8 Weeks
Platform
Web Dashboard
Tools
Figma, Hotjar, FigJam
The Problem

People open their banking app, feel anxious, and close it immediately.

FinTrack's existing dashboard displayed raw transaction data in a chronological list — no categorization, no visual context, no actionable insight. Hotjar session recordings showed 78% of users spending less than 40 seconds on the dashboard before leaving. Exit surveys revealed a consistent theme: "I can see numbers but I don't know what to do with them."

The startup had strong transaction data infrastructure but was losing users at the dashboard step. My goal was to transform raw financial data into a clear, motivating interface that made users feel in control — not anxious — about their money.

"I check my bank account the same way I check the scale — quickly, then I try to forget what I saw." — Diary study participant, age 27

Research & Insights

Understanding the emotional relationship with money

I ran a 7-day diary study with 8 participants, asking them to record every time they checked their finances and how they felt before and after. I also conducted 6 contextual interviews to observe how people actually interact with existing tools like Mint, YNAB, and Apple Card's spending view.

The key insight: users didn't need more data. They needed the right data, framed positively, with clear next actions. I defined a design principle — "progress over perfection" — that became the north star for every decision.

Design Process

Turning anxiety into clarity

I sketched three dashboard concepts — a card-based layout, a chart-first layout, and a goals-first layout. Card-based won in preference testing because it let users see one thing at a time without being overwhelmed. I then built wireframes in Figma, establishing a visual hierarchy: monthly summary → category breakdown → individual transactions → savings goal progress.

Color was a deliberate choice: green for positive momentum, a neutral warm gray for neutral spending, and a soft amber (never red) for over-budget categories. Red is associated with danger and loss — exactly the emotional state we were trying to prevent.

Testing & Outcomes

From 40 seconds to 3 minutes of engagement

After deploying the redesigned dashboard to a beta group of 22 users, I tracked session duration, feature interaction, and ran a post-test survey measuring perceived confidence around finances.

The results validated the design direction. Average session duration increased from 38 seconds to 3.2 minutes. The budget feature — previously invisible — was interacted with by 71% of users in the first session. Most significantly, self-reported financial confidence improved from 2.8/5 to 4.1/5 after 2 weeks of use.
